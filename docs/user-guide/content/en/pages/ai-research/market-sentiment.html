Market Sentiment

Page · AI Research · 5 min

FinBERT analyzes news and social media sentiment in real time, giving each asset a score from -1 to +1 on a color-coded heatmap — helping you confirm or question your structural signals.

TL;DR

The Market Sentiment page shows real-time sentiment analysis for major trading pairs, powered by the FinBERT model. At the center is a Fear & Greed gauge. Below it, a sentiment heatmap — rows are trading pairs, colors gradient from red (negative) through neutral to green (positive), each cell showing the exact score. You can also paste a news headline or tweet to get a FinBERT breakdown of positive/neutral/negative proportions.

Who uses it & when

Every trader should check sentiment before entering a position — not to make the decision, but to test your thesis. When your technical bullish signal coincides with heavily negative market sentiment, you need a stronger entry case. When sentiment is extreme (Fear & Greed below 10 or above 90), markets have already priced it in — trading contrarily at extremes carries high risk.

Page Anatomy

  1. ① Fear & Greed Gauge — Overall market Fear & Greed index (0-100) on a semicircular gauge. Needle color gradients from red (fear) to green (greed). The numeric value and zone label (Extreme Fear / Fear / Neutral / Greed / Extreme Greed) are shown below.
  2. ② Sentiment Heatmap — One row per major trading pair, showing FinBERT sentiment score (-1.0 to +1.0). Color-coded: green (+0.2+ = positive), blue (-0.2 to +0.2 = neutral), red (-0.2 or below = negative). The bar length on the right reflects sentiment intensity.

Reading the Key Metrics

  • Fear & Greed Index — Below 20 is Extreme Fear, above 80 is Extreme Greed. Historically, extreme fear often presents buying opportunities, while extreme greed may signal a top. But don't blindly fade extremes — markets can stay extreme longer than you can stay solvent.
  • Sentiment Score — FinBERT's -1 to +1 composite score per asset. Positive = news and social media are generally bullish. Negative = generally bearish. Higher absolute values mean stronger sentiment.
  • Sentiment vs. Structural Alignment — The most important use: when sentiment aligns with your structural analysis direction, increase conviction. When they conflict, either wait for sentiment to shift or require stronger structural evidence before entering.

Common Actions, Step by Step

  1. Open Market Sentiment and scan the Fear & Greed gauge to understand the overall market mood.
  2. Browse the sentiment heatmap to find your assets of interest and their scores.
  3. Compare the sentiment score against the signal direction from your AI Research Room or structural analysis. Aligned? Add conviction. Conflicting? Proceed with caution.
  4. Paste a news headline or tweet into the "Text Analysis" area and tap "Analyze" for a FinBERT breakdown of positive/neutral/negative proportions.
  5. Publish the analysis result as a signal with one click — useful for quickly generating a short signal when a major negative story breaks.
  6. In your daily trading loop, check the sentiment page first to gauge the market's tone, then dive into specific asset analysis.

Behind the Scenes

Sentiment data comes from the FinBERTAdapter in sentiment_finbert.py. FinBERT is a BERT-based financial sentiment analysis model (ProsusAI/finbert) fine-tuned on financial news text. Analysis outputs probabilities for positive, negative, and neutral labels; the final sentiment score = positive probability - negative probability (range -1 to +1). When model loading fails, it gracefully degrades to keyword matching (using predefined positive/negative word lists). Batch analysis uses the analyze_batch method for parallel processing. Sentiment results can be pushed to the Signal Center via the "Publish as Signal" feature, where they enter the fusion scoring system as a sentiment-category signal.
