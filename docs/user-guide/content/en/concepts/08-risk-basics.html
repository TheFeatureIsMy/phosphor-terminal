Risk Basics

Concept · 08 / 10 · 6 min

Risk management isn't "be careful" — it's a concrete set of numbers: max loss per trade, total exposure, how many losses before you stop.

Why It Matters

Even a great strategy loses. Losing isn't the problem; the problem is "one big loss wiping out ten small wins" or "losing the capital you need for the next trade." Risk management installs layered guardrails: stop-loss per trade, drawdown per day, circuit breaker over time. As long as no single layer fails alone, you stay in the game.

Professional traders think in R-multiples. 1R = the dollar amount you're willing to risk on this trade. If a trade risks -1R and targets +2R, a 40% win rate still nets you money long-term. Every risk rule is ultimately about defending that R.

Key Metrics

  • Position Size — usually 1%–2% of account per trade. Cranking it to 5% sounds bolder, but it turns -1R into -5% of equity. Four losing trades in a row = -20%.
  • Stop Loss — defined as a price or ATR multiple, executed automatically on touch. A "mental stop" is no stop.
  • Max Drawdown — the largest peak-to-trough equity drop. A -8% intraday drawdown usually means strategy and regime are misaligned: stop for the day, review.
  • Circuit Breaker — N consecutive losses, daily loss over threshold, abnormal volatility — any of these triggers a full halt. Once set, don't override manually.

Common Pitfalls

  • "Just hold a bit longer" is how small losses become big ones. A triggered stop is a triggered stop.
  • Treating the backtest's max drawdown as a ceiling. Live is almost always harder than backtest. Leave 2x buffer.
  • Running N strategies on the same 2% risk budget. Exposures add up: five strategies at 2% each = 10%.
  • Restarting immediately after a breaker. The breaker exists to make you stop and think — review before resuming.
