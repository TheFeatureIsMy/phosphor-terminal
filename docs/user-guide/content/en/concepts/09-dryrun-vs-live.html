Backtest vs Dry-Run vs Live

Concept · 09 / 10 · 5 min

The same strategy runs in three worlds: backtest checks history, dry-run checks the present, live checks your wallet. Small gaps between the three are normal — big ones mean don't ship.
"Backtesting is the process of testing a trading strategy on historical data to see how it would have performed. It's the first step before risking real capital."— Freqtrade[1]

Why It Matters

Every trader wants to skip testing and go straight to live. Nobody who did that has historically survived. The three phases aren't bureaucracy — they're three independent filters: backtest filters strategies that are mathematically unprofitable; dry-run filters strategies that are overfit (great on history, broken on the next time window); small-size live filters strategies that work on paper but you can't psychologically handle. Only strategies that pass all three deserve scale.

The realistic part: there's always a gap between backtest and live (slippage, fees, fill latency, thin liquidity). That gap has a name: backtest-live gap. A gap you can reproduce in dry-run is a "known gap" and acceptable. A strategy that backtests +20% but loses -5% live has too much gap — don't ship.

The Three Phases

  • Backtest — Simulate orders on historical candles, runs years of data in minutes. Strengths: fast, reproducible. Weaknesses: look-ahead bias (you know which candle wins), no slippage (assumes you fill at desired price), parameter overfitting from tuning on the same data.
  • Dry-Run (Paper Trading) — Use live market data, but route orders to a paper account. Eliminates every form of backtest cheating (no peeking) and exposes engineering bugs (disconnects, rate limits, stale data). Weakness: no real fills, so liquidity issues stay hidden.
  • Live — Real capital. Even after everything else, start with tiny size (0.5%–2% of account) so real slippage, partial fills, and psychological pressure all surface. Run 2 weeks without blow-ups before scaling.

Common Pitfalls

  • Tuning and evaluating on the same data — textbook overfitting. Use train / validation / test splits; touch the test set once.
  • Backtesting with 0 slippage and 0 fees — lying to yourself. Add real exchange fees (Binance spot ≈ 0.1%) and sensible slippage (0.05%–0.2%).
  • Calling 2 days of dry-run "enough" — most strategies need 1–2 weeks to see all market states (trend / chop / shock).
  • Going full-size live immediately — backtest's worst drawdown is a historical maximum; live almost always exceeds it.
