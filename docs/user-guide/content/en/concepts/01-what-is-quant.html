What Is Quant Trading

Concept · 01 / 10 · 5 min

Quant trading means writing your decision criteria as code and letting the machine execute, not your gut.
"Quantitative trading uses mathematical models and historical data to make trading decisions, removing emotion from the process."— Investopedia[1]

Why It Matters

The biggest advantage of quant trading isn't "more money" — it's discipline. Human decisions are driven by fear, greed, and FOMO. Code isn't. You set the rules, it follows them. It won't open a position at 3 AM on a whim.

The other underrated benefit is verifiability. A strategy that fails backtesting won't magically work live. Most discretionary traders never know their actual win rate. Quant traders know exactly how every strategy has performed historically — every P&L tick is recorded.

How To Spot One

"Quant" sounds intimidating, but it's really three steps:

  1. Define rules — What triggers a buy? A sell? Position size? Stop loss? Write them as clear "if... then..." statements.
  2. Validate — Run the rules against historical data (backtest) to see how they would have performed.
  3. Execute — Connect the validated rules to the live market and let the system trade.

Every quant system, no matter how complex, follows the same loop: Strategy → Signal → Order.

Common Pitfalls

  • Over-optimization — A backtest that looks too good is usually a red flag. Curve-fitting to historical data guarantees failure in live markets. Keep it simple.
  • Ignoring slippage and fees — A profitable backtest can become a losing strategy after costs. Always include realistic transaction costs.
  • No risk management — Most beginners focus on "how to make money" instead of "how to not lose money." Risk first, strategy second.
  • Bugs — The biggest enemy of a quant trader isn't the market; it's your own code. Test thoroughly before going live.
